Here I am standing along the familiar shoreline of the Yodogawa river in Osaka, bike in hand and ready to set off on a 100km cycle up past Kyoto and back. I have an itch that needs scratching and I want to remind myself of the torture this river has put me through before.

---

With 7 days to fill before our group reconvened in Fukuoka, on the other side of the country after all departing Tokyo, I was alone and had set myself a challenge of spending the week pedalling around the shores of Lake Biwa, Japan's largest lake. While the rest of the group had collectively decided to spend the week in the cities, enjoying the life of the trains, the conbinis, and the ease of life, I would spend each day in the desolate rural countryside of Japan just cycling away and working my ass off.

The idea started off the back of a friend's journey a few years before, a bike ride from Tokyo to Osaka and back over 14 days. I was always amazed by it, and wanted to mimic a similar trip at some point in my life. After a couple years and a few more trips to and from Japan, I decided that this trip would be the perfect opportunity for me to do something similar. As a group we would be visiting a few cities in Japan however the middle of three weeks we would spend as a "Dealer's Week", a week where we would all split up and find our own way from Tokyo and all meet up at the end in Fukuoka.

My plan was a loop around Lake Biwa, or Biwako, around the Biwaichi cycling path that circled the lake. A round trip of about 200km, plus getting to and from there from Osaka, and a couple detours each day would total closer to 350km. So I mapped out a route starting from Osaka, heading up through Kyoto and to Otsu where I would then cycle counter-clockwise around the lake along the marked cycling path, cross over the mountains to the northern shore of the country in Tsuruga, and then back along the lake continuing around to where I started before heading back to Osaka to safely return the bike to the same shop I rented it from originally. With everything mapped out I booked all of the accommodations I needed for the route and booked in the bike and a couple months later I was in Osaka the morning of my first day, ready to head off.

With everything I thought I needed to survive a week packed into two panniers either side of my back wheel, I headed up north along the Yodogawa river, naively ready for a smooth 7 day cycle. The cycle up to Kyoto was relatively flat and rather unchallenging other than a few small hills along the way. Although by the time I reached Kyoto I had realised my first challenge. Although the path around the lake was fully marked making it hard to get lost, there is no marked cycling path from Osaka to Otsu, and here I was in Kyoto having just followed the Yodogawa river as far as it took me. I was now in an industrial part of town and clearly not on course to where I needed to go. I checked maps and got myself back on track, a reroute that would add about 20km to the day putting me at 70km. This ended up being my longest day because thankfully I managed to not get myself lost on later days.

However, once I pulled out of Kyoto I realised my next mistake in the (mis)planning. The hills. As it turns out there's a mountain range that separates Kyoto and Otsu and the only way for me to make it to the other side was to climb over these mountains. A climb I really wasn't prepared for. With a reminder that I had 6 more days of this to come, I accepted fate and put everything I had into grinding those gears. I was barely moving, crawling up the mountain as the cars slowly passed me sharing the single lane, though in typical Japanese polite fashion not one of them vented their true frustration. Everything I had to stay put in the seat and not give up to walk the bike. Everything I had to climb what was the biggest climb I'd ever done, one that still remains the biggest to this day. Absolutely exhausted I made it to the top of that mountain, or at least the passing, and ever so gently floated back down the other side into Otsu, catching up on the breath I had just lost.

After a gentle arrival into Otsu I was absolutely hammered. I wasn't planning on any day being this hard, despite only being 70km, when you add in the climbs I was destroyed. I arrived into Otsu a little earlier than check-in was, as it was only a small guesthouse they had a later check-in time so I thought I'd use this time to just ride along the lake, admiring the sheer size of it, and imagining myself riding around its edge distantly into the slowly disappearing shoreline. I went for a ride along from where the guesthouse was, into town and where the cruise terminal is. As this was a heavily pedestrianised part of town the path was all concreted and flat which made for an easy ride.

That was until I rode the bike along the edge of two concrete slabs that were slightly off-level, and just enough to notice my back wheel move sideways ever so slightly. Enough to feel that something was up. I got off and looked down and sure enough I'd given myself a flat. Nice. I had a spare tube in the under-seat pouch that came with the bike so I wasn't too worried. I would walk the bike back to the guesthouse and then worry about fixing it then. I'm no stranger to replacing a tube so I wasn't worried about this whole ordeal. I walked the bike back, checked in, they asked if the bike outside was mine and if I wanted to store it, I accepted. Now being a guesthouse, there wasn't a lot of room here so the bike ended up staying in their garage which was already quite packed. This put a little dent in my plan to fix the tube that night but I figured I'd just fix it in the morning after I checked out.

I climbed up the small staircase to my room, found my bed and just completely passed out. My original plan was to ride to each town, explore around for a bit, grab some dinner, and then go to bed. But I was absolutely wrecked. I forced myself to get back out of bed at around 9 and go and find some ramen to eat for dinner before everything shut and I left myself starving. After a hearty bowl of ramen, satisfied I headed back for some rest. With the climbs having destroyed my legs, I was really rethinking my plan to ride from the lake up to Tsuruga because this would involve another set of climbs on consecutive days that I really wasn't looking forward to. I decided to cancel on this leg, and just stay in Makino for two days instead. A decision that would later save me. Morning came around and I checked out, thanked them for the space and had a bit of a chat and then I grabbed my bike from their garage before heading out.

It's raining, I've already checked out and I still need a place to fix the wheel. I find a park across the road with some small amount of cover and quickly run for it, trying not to get too wet. I turn the bike on its seat and get to work taking the wheel off, pulling the tyre off, taking the tube out and seeing where it was flat. Right along a seam, which is the worst place to try and repair because the lift of the seam also lifts any patches you put on it so it's likely to just slowly leak over time. With that in mind I just replace the tube with the spare that I had been given. That's exactly what the tube is for but leaves me with an issue, that I now no longer have a spare tube to fix any more flats that come up so I just need to hope for my best luck.

I get back on the bike, the rain has started to clear up now and I make my way to Higashiomi, constantly looking for somewhere to buy some more tubes as I'm acutely aware that I'm basically riding naked and unprotected. With no train stations along the way I ride as carefully as I can. After a few hours I make it there alive, though with no tubes in hand still. Today's ride is where civilisation starts to thin out, the houses and shops slowly disappearing, and along with that the bike shops also cease to exist. Though now with a day's ride without any issues, maybe, I can just put it off until tomorrow, I mean I'd have to.

The next day I head up towards Nagahama but with a plan. I'd be passing by Hikone, a city not large by any means but big enough to have its own castle and by that metric hopefully big enough to have a bike shop too. I cycle there, stop in at the castle and spend lunch there having a wander around. While I'm stopped I search up nearby shops and thankfully there's a single shop a few blocks ride away. I hop back on and ride straight for the shop. Much to my relief I find plenty of spare tubes and check out with two, a pair and a spare. Just in case I need to use one of the tubes, I'll still have a spare and won't be stressed out in the sparse countryside with no plan B.

The rest of the ride to Nagahama was rather unspectacular. It was at this point the silence of the empty plains really sank in. I would go an hour without passing any cars and there were certainly no other cyclists that I'd pass. It was just me and the bike. I had no more issues with the bike that day and so checked in for the night. With no stresses I left in the morning headed for the next town of Makino. This ride was also quite uneventful, and other than a few small climbs I reached Makino in a short time thanks to the day's short ride. I got there a little early for check-in and so I went for a bit of a ride around the town, there was apparently a nice tree-lined avenue that wasn't too far away so I went to go check it out.

Now being a small town, and riding the back roads off to this avenue, the roads are not the most routinely maintained you've seen. No real cycling infrastructure and the concrete and tar was pot-filled and cracked everywhere, just enough to make a ride a little uncomfortable. Apparently also enough to give you a flat tyre. Great. I mean I'd just seen the roads and their poor condition so this one's really on me, I should've just gotten off and walked. I find a park nearby with a little gazebo and walk the bike in a walk of shame. I prop the bike on its seat and get to work. After some practice just two days before I get this one replaced pretty quickly and get back on my way. I bought two spares for a reason and so I still have insurance and I'm not worried.

I head back for the avenue. I get maybe 100 metres and feel a certain softness I'm all too familiar with. I look down at the back tyre, flat. Even greater. Probably a bad seating or something in the tyre that I should've checked for. I walk back to the park, insurance in hand, and get to work replacing the tube in the same gazebo I was just in. With a second round of practice I get this replaced in no time. Eager to see this avenue before the sun sets I jet back off again.

This time I make it 50 metres, soft, another flat. I look down and sure enough my back wheel is now flat for the fourth time, the third in less than an hour. This time I'm really screwed, I've just used up my pair and a spare, and after checking, the flat is on the seam again, a patch that won't last a whole day. I'm basically stranded in this small town, with no bike shop in sight, and no way to ride out of this ride.

With not much to really do in this town I'm not really sure why I chose this as the place to stay an extra day, but nonetheless it worked in my favour because I now had a whole day to do something to get me out of this situation. I spent the spare day shamefully catching the once-an-hour train all the way down to Kyoto where I knew I could find many bike shops and would certainly be able to get a spare tube. I found a well reviewed shop in the city, did my research on the correct tube size to buy, suspecting this was my reason for two consecutive flats, and headed back for Makino. The full trip took my whole day from this town, but being back in the city with people and things happening after a few days of quiet and silence was refreshing.

On the second morning I checked out and went to work on the bike in the middle of the carpark. I once again was down to one spare, but I had a working wheel once again, and putting confidence in myself I just hoped everything would be fine from here on out. It was. I headed back down to Otsu and as a mark of accomplishment found my way back to the starting point where I'd been crouched over the bike in the rain in a park four days prior. The next day I headed for the hills back towards Osaka, the same hills I encountered on day one.

I walked them this time. After cancelling a leg of the trip that would've taken me to either side of the country, being crouched over the bike repairing it in a park in the rain, getting three consecutive flats in an hour, and spending a full day catching trains going into the city instead of cycling in rural countryside, I really didn't feel like I had much to prove. I just wanted to finish. I had friends who were already in Fukuoka that were waiting for me to arrive to share my stories over a few beers.

Finally back in Osaka I returned the bike to the store I rented it from. The lady noticed the tube missing from the pouch and asked if I'd got a flat, "Yeah I got _a_ flat", too ashamed to tell her about the other three, and after getting so many, I hadn't been bothered to try and fold them back into the pouch, instead just throwing them into my panniers in a fit.

Absolutely wrecked and exhausted after the last week I made my way to Osaka Station, more ready than ever to catch the Shinkansen and land in Fukuoka. I made it there some hours later, the rest of the group already at the pub by the station a few drinks deep. I left my suitcase outside, and met them at a table, and relieved myself with a beer and stories.

---

So here I am back on the river, ready for a long cycle back to Kyoto, though this time I'm limiting myself to a single out and back, just to experience it one more time.
